# Man-made National Park Ecosystem Summary

## Overview
This document summarizes the ecosystem and biodiversity discussion for a man-made national park with unclear natural context.

### Ecosystem context
- Designed landscape and/or restored habitat.
- Likely to include mixed natural and anthropogenic elements.
- Could contain planted forests, meadows, wetlands, ponds, and urban green infrastructure.
- Visitor facilities, trails, and buffers influence microhabitats.

## Key ecosystem compartments
- Trees/shrub canopy (planted or regrown)
- Grasses and forbs (pollinator meadows)
- Water bodies (lakes, ponds, streams, constructed wetlands)
- Soil and microbial community (restoration soils, compost)
- Rocks and artificial structures (shelter for amphibians/invertebrates)

## Biodiversity profile (likely)
- Plants: native grasses, wildflowers, shrubs, shade trees, pioneer species.
- Insects: pollinators (bees, butterflies), beetles, dragonflies, other macroinvertebrates.
- Birds: generalists, migratory stopovers, insectivores, waterfowl (if water features exist).
- Small mammals: rodents, rabbits, urban-adapted carnivores (fox, raccoon), depending on region.
- Amphibians/reptiles: frogs, toads in wetlands, lizards/snakes if suitable habitat exists.
- Aquatic life: macroinvertebrates, fish in artificial lakes, tadpoles.
- Fungi/microbes: decomposers and mycorrhizal networks supporting soil health.

## Drivers of biodiversity in a managed park
- Planting choices (native vs exotic species).
- Habitat connectivity and overall area.
- Water management and wetland quality.
- Maintenance regime (mowing cycle, pesticide usage, invasive species control).
- Visitor management to reduce disturbance while enabling education.
